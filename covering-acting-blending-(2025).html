Covering // Acting // Blending

During the later stages of the bubonic plague in Europe – the black death if you want to add some dramatics to it – specialised physicians began wearing outfits and masks made of leather that completely covered the face and body. You’ve probably seen them before. They’ve got the glass lenses over the eyes and the long beaks that droop away from the head. They often put dried flowers or herbs in the beaks, anything aromatic that they thought might have been able to cleanse the air as they worked with the sick.

Cut forward around 400 years and we can see how those creepy plague doctors were kind of on to something. The leather of the clothes was wicked with wax that prevented moisture, and they stitched the legs into their boots to prevent any openings. In spite of germ theory not arriving for another one to two hundred years, they knew that some air was dangerous to breathe, and even carried a rod around to keep infected patients what they believed to be a safe distance away.

The beaks aren’t quite the N-95s of our modern practitioners, but a physical barrier to filter sickness out of the air isn’t too dissimilar. The only real difference is that the idea has been honed and improved as knowledge and science revealed the true dangers of disease to us, knowledge and science we only really came to understand because these plague doctors were willing to perform autopsies on the dead. And from centuries of research stemming there, we were able to develop a new mask for a new millennium’s new plague.

Society reacted quite poorly when these new masks were enforced, though. So much so that it became political to wear one. Suspicious. Tense looks were shot between the people that wore them and the people that didn’t, side eyes and judgy stares. Like the sharks and the jets if one side had their glasses all fogged up and the other was a bit wheezy.

I probably shouldn’t have been surprised by the way society reacted to masks. After all, the plague doctor – hell, masks in general – had long been a laughable image. In spite of the surprisingly apt measures taken to produce and wear what amounts to a medieval hazmat suit, they became a recognisable figure during the 1600s, and that recognisability saw the same beaked plague masks become the hilarious stock character of Il Dottore.

Stock characters were the barebones of Commedia dell’arte, a form of theatre that became particularly prevalent in Italy between the 16th and 18th centuries. It was largely improvisational, with lines and scenes being made up as the shows progressed, different across most performances, even within the same troupe of performers. The stock characters, however, were archetypal characters referred to by the masks that embodied those characters. They were representations and caricatures of professions and regionalities of the time. Il Capitano, for example, was the braggadocious Spanish soldier, while Il Dottore was the well-learned, wordy, and often pedantic scholar from Bologna. While the details of all the masks shifted with the characters as the cultural context of each performance developed them, Il Dottore always bore an emphasis of the nose, typically beak-like or bulbous. This was the immediate legacy of the plague doctors.

I wasn’t particularly well versed in history or drama when I first encountered Commedia dell’arte in year 8, though. What struck me then was how negative the portrayal of these masks was considering their lofty professions, and in contrast, how favourable some of the lower class stock characters were. Referred to as the zanni, these were the practical jokers and clowns of Commedia. While they often were flawed characters, their defining feature was their ability to escape punishment or problem through cleverness, exuberance, or luck, as opposed to the smug or self-centred masks, who were often trapped by their own defining flaws within the jokes played on them by the zanni. This built-in critique of lofty professions and simultaneous exhibition of the street smarts of the lower class was not to last, however, and saw a decline along with the renaissance in Italy.

The legacy of masks was not lost, however. The breaking down of social hierarchies in particular was carried forth into the use of masks for extravagant masquerade balls.

I had always thought of masquerades as affairs of the rich, the upper crust that would soon find their heads in baskets as democracy swelled in the streets of France. But as it turns out, they were present throughout Europe and – especially early in their prominence – attendees were not exclusively nobility and royalty. When they were first brought to England around the early 1700s, for instance, any who could afford a ticket were allowed to attend, and one of the great affordances of the masks all attendees wore was the obscuring of class.

In some places, this universality remained, but in others, indeed including pre-revolutionary France, the evolution of the masquerade was toward elitism. The temptation to blend in with the crowd and go undiscovered was apparently a big draw card for royalty such as Marie Antoinette, but invitations to her events were only typically extended to other royals, the nobility, and on occasion, through sheer generosity, to the ultra wealthy members of the bourgeoisie.

An interesting aspect of Marie Antoinette’s masquerades was that it became almost a tradition for everybody to unmask at the end of the night, so everybody could see who was under what mask. This seems like it would defeat the purpose of the night, but then the point of the event in these contexts wasn’t anonymity, it was facade. Nobody recognised you in the exact moment you were engaging in the unthinkable debauchery of the time – how scandalous, a woman, flirting – but then you didn’t recognise anybody else in their own immodesty, either.

It didn’t matter. After all, to be at a masquerade ball during this period of French history was a marker of just how high up in the food chain you were. Such a marked difference from the leveling of society that masquerades were once said to have, even if it was as imaginary as it was temporary.

All the same, the meaning of a mask had changed again. From the practical protective elements of the plague doctor to the distinct identifying nature of masks in Commedia dell’arte it had transformed once more into an anonymising feature.

Beyond each of these instances, the mask I find most intriguing is the one that acts to perform each of the functions already mentioned at the same time.

Masking is a behaviour common amongst neurodivergent people, involving putting on an act and suppressing some of their neurodivergent qualities to help them fit in more with neurotypical people. It’s a terrible, terrible thing to learn. In the short term it costs a lot of energy, and can build a lot of stress or depression in a masking neurodivergent person. Over a longer period, masking can lead to some serious psychological problems, including burnout, imposter syndrome, and even the feeling of losing your identity.

But many don’t know that will be the outcome when they start masking. Many are just kids at first. You don’t know what’s causing it, but you recognise that something is going wrong, some of your behaviours get worse responses than others. So, you protect yourself. You filter out the behaviours that get a negative response, behaviours you only know to cover up because you've experienced the severity of the response, the bullying, the mockery. And from long periods of observation, you can finally develop something that will actually protect you.

Then it’s just a matter of wearing the mask, putting on the act. Emphasise the parts of yourself that others want to see. You might need to modify this from person to person, context to context. Don’t let that stop you. It’s development, it’s growth, just make sure you’ve always got the defining features that everybody expects when they see you. On occasion, you might be trapped by the mask you wear in the situations placed upon you by others. Try not to panic. Just develop the mask further, it’ll all be okay.

Soon enough, you’ll seem as neurotypical as the rest of them. You’ll blend right in, no matter the context. At some point, you might be tempted to unmask, and let everybody see who you are underneath. But this would defeat the purpose of it all. At that point, you’re so far into the mask that it might not even be about anonymity anymore, but about the facade itself. Do you know who you are under the mask?

No, better just to keep it on. You’ll be seen as normal, level with the rest of society. It doesn’t particularly matter if it’s imaginary or temporary. For the stretch of time you need the mask on, you’ll have it. You’ll be safe.
