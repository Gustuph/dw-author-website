Part 1: Magnitude

Magnitude scales for earthquakes are the kind of thing you think about in the abstract. The grief, the withdrawal, the pain and loss following an earthquake doesn’t matter to Richter. It has disdain for its effect on us humans. A stupid scale doesn’t care who or what you lose. It doesn’t warn you how much it can hurt. Which leaves me here. Hurting. And all I can blame is the number 8. No more jokes made at my expense when I misplace a word in a sentence. No more laughter or groans at my dumbest puns. Nobody to tease about their truly awful football team.

No more Leon.


It took days before the building was declared safe enough for me to go in and clean up. Nobody else could make it to his place. It was left with me to comb through his apartment and figure out what to do with what remained. The whole house was dusty. The main room had benchtop appliances and a shattered plate on the floor. A sheet of plastic tarp and tape kept the cold air from entering through the hole in the back corner of the bedroom.

The mattress had been taken from the broken bed frame, both ends bent inward from where the slats were crushed under rubble. A rust-coloured stain laid underneath it.


I decided to start with the combined kitchen/dining/living space. Leon saved up all he could to get out of this place. He always joked about what a luxury it must be to have a room exclusively for watching TV. Like a toilet for the mind.

The plate on the floor wasn’t the only dish that broke. A few of the glasses shattered in their cupboard, and I had to be careful removing and inspecting each one. I took my time with it, relishing the mindless task. I could have been looking through these glasses for any reason. I could have been at a garage sale, checking out a new set. Not that I go to garage sales all that often, Leon was much thriftier than me. But the longer I took to package the intact glasses and line the good plates with butcher’s paper as I loaded them into little boxes, the longer it would be before I had to move to the only other two rooms in the dingy little place.

So I compared power plugs to appliances with trial and error, wrapped each cord delicately around its match. I meticulously labelled each box and perfectly taped down each corner. I could even rationalise that it was so everything was taken care of to the best of my ability. I said it out loud a few times, hoping it would convince me that I wasn’t just a coward.


The lounge set was barely worth scavenging. The TV itself, not wall-mounted like I had constantly suggested it should be, was screen-down on the floor. The cables had pulled taut over the surface of the unit, still attached to a power board hooked onto the back. When I lifted the TV into an upright position, I noticed a frame had fallen under it. I picked it up with a shaking hand and a sound came from deep in my throat. He was holding me, arms around me, his warmest hoodie obscuring most of my body from the camera. My face was beaming, though. Eyes closed, grin wide, teeth catching the flash. His nose was buried in the pompom atop my beanie, but his eyes were wrinkled at the corners, his eyebrows almost surprised in the way they always got when he laughed. His hair fell around both of our ears, long enough to tickle at the spot above my neck.

I stopped there for the day.


The bathroom was a simple affair. The inspector had said the lack of external doors or windows had kept it relatively untouched. Leon being the neat freak that he was helped too, the benches had been cleared of most things already. The only change to this room from before the earthquake was the plastic cup that contained his toothpaste and toothbrush laying on the floor. That and the constant dripping that sounded from the shower, a building-wide issue stemming from impacts to the already questionable plumbing.

I wasn’t satisfied with just packing everything away and moving on though, so I took inspiration from him and decided to give the entire room a thorough clean. It didn’t need it, but I scrubbed hard at the plastic and the porcelain until it all shone in the same stark white. I expunged every miniscule spot of soap grime off of the shower’s glass door. I broke out the dust buster to vacuum the hair that had gathered in a hard-to-reach corner behind the toilet and I mopped every tile of the floor.

It was really only a couple hours of work though. Only a couple of hours before I had to turn my attention to what I could logically recognise I had been avoiding.


The closet was a mess. I tried to swallow my glee at opening the door and letting the containers that once were neatly arranged on a high shelf come tumbling out to my feet. The guilt of celebrating at the sight ate up any positivity I felt. I collapsed to my knees more than knelt and started organising and cataloguing it all.

The tubs held a shortened lifetime’s worth of physical memories. Developed photos from disposable cameras were packed into yellow envelopes. School workbooks and textbooks held scribbled drawings in the margins and the slow evidence of progress toward his pen license. An old school shirt was balled up and stuffed down the side, and I couldn’t decipher the feeling it evoked when I unfurled it.

The wrinkles were heavily set into the scratchy, starchy fabric, but the more obvious tainting of the pure white shirt was the marker scrawled all over it. Brief messages from forgotten friends were shakily inked onto the short-sleeved button-up, well wishes and inside jokes and assurances they’d keep in touch, along with a couple spots where tears had run through the words.

My name was alone on his breast pocket surrounded by a heart. And on the back of his collar. And again down by his right hip. I remember being so worried we were tracking in different directions, on different life paths. I held on so tightly to him that day.


Sometime during the sorting of his life, I had donned the high school bomber jacket that had been half buried beneath a pile of old books in one tub. I had worn it often when we first started going out, but it felt much smaller on me now. It didn’t smell like him either, which stung at a part of me I didn’t recognise. Or maybe it just smelled like he did back then.

The realisation that one day I’d forget how he smelled was softened by the cologne I found in his bedside table. It had been cushioned from the destruction by his overabundance of neatly folded socks.

I packed the rest of the table away and then inspected the cologne further. The glass bottle was half empty and a finger smudge marred the faux silver of the dispenser. I couldn’t resist matching how he must have held it, spritzing a little into the air around me.

I watched the particles drift in the still room. They swirled as I breathed and twinkled in the sun that filtered through the tarp. I was transfixed by them, watching them dance through the air until they came to land on the carpet at my feet, disappearing one by one into the plain brown floor. I followed the last few down, sitting cross legged by the ruined remainder of his bed. When I couldn’t pick out any more in the air, my eyes found themselves caught on the rough circle of redder brown.

I shifted further onto the floor as the tears came, laying on the rough carpet and staring at the stain. I let the sobs wrack through my chest, let my stomach clench as I trembled, a finger touching gently at the edge of the mark.


Part 2: Aftershocks

For all the terror of an earthquake, the threat of aftershocks means you can’t relax in the assurance that the danger is over. The panic stays in your bones, waiting to ignite with the first shaking steps, keeping you from coming out of your hiding place, from escaping the rubble, from cleaning the remains. There’s always that heat in your veins, that red-hot adrenaline that makes you fearful of the next impact. So how long do you wait? When does everything stop shaking?


The key was icy in my hands. The horizon ran red behind me. The warmth of the afternoon sun was skin deep where it splashed across my back. My shadow was silhouetted on the door and beside it, I could see his. Taller, rocking back on his heels, face in immaculate profile as he looked somewhere behind me. My fingers were bone white where they clutched at the metal, and the breeze blew a chill where it flowed over my clammy skin. His shadow tilted toward me, his head leaning down towards mine, and I felt more than heard Miles whisper in my ear.

“I’d hate to rush you, but we’re running out of daylight here.”

I blushed, heat shooting through my body at the words. I liked him being that close to me. He got closer still when he kissed me on the top of my head. Everything shook inside me and my stomach melted into butterflies. I wrenched my sweat-slick fingers around the key and shoved my shoulder into the door, throwing a smile at him that I hoped looked bashful enough to cover up my terror. The door groaned in surprise at my unusual force, and I tumbled over the threshold in a calamity of limbs. I begged with my whole being that he would find the panic endearing. That he saw scatterbrained as cute. That my body would stop vibrating.

The living room I half-fell into was neat and tidy, just as I’d left it. I’d spent enough time mopping the floors for them to shine the night before. My feet rushed me toward the kitchen, a destination, any destination, that might keep me from looking him in the eye.

“Can I get you anything? Coffee, tea…?”

“Water’s fine.”

I flitted to the cupboard for glasses and back to the tap, flicking it on and waiting for the pipes to cool. I leant hard against the bench, letting it support my weight. I risked a glance towards him. He was politely glancing at my cookbooks and mugs, all tastefully arranged. Raking his eyes over the splashback I’d scrubbed to spotlessness. He looked at me and smiled. A pretty smile. The heat in my cheeks grew as a grin split my lips. I cleared my throat and turned back to the tap, running a finger under the still warm stream.

“Sorry, the water takes forever to cool down…”

“No worries.”

He was closer than I realised and I recoiled in shock. I jolted and span, and his hand found my hip and I was stable.

Everything was still. My heart barely beat, chest rising and falling in agonising breaths, a deep, honeyed pause that fell and settled into a still, calm moment.

Sunlight through the kitchen window glinted off the frame of his glasses. He had pretty eyes as well, gentle and focused through the lenses, holding me, keeping my gaze. I reached out and turned off the tap, glasses forgotten by the sink.

He moved his hand up my body, hip to waist to back to neck. I moved us out of the kitchen, through the living room to my bedroom. A gentle warmth soaked into my muscles. He was careful to avoid anything too scandalous. I was hungry for him to get tangled around me. The mattress was soft enough that I didn’t feel bad when we tumbled onto the coverlet, hands quickly tangling in his hair and his collar.

I pushed harder into him, wishing he’d push back. Instead, he pulled away, breaking the kiss. The heat was gone, flushed from my system by stilted, gasping breaths. I heard him mumble an apology, but I couldn’t take it in, turning and sitting on the edge of the bed so he wouldn’t see the tears rapidly pooling in my eyes.

“Can I get you a tissue?”

Shit.

“Yeah. Yes, the um… in the living room.”

He was going slow, just like I had asked him to. But I didn’t want to deal with slow. Slow made me sit in these moments, sit in the mess of emotions I was exposing Miles to. I moved back to lean against the pillows piled up along the headboard, knees pulled close to my chest. The tears kept falling, running lines down my face and wetting my shirt and hands and bedsheets. He came back into the room and handed me the box. There was no pity in his eyes. Just patience. Calm.

The bed was slim, and he wrapped an arm around my shoulder to sit next to me, rubbing his hand along my upper back as I buried my face in a tissue. His presence was comforting and after a time, I fought to compose myself, readying myself to face him, to apologise and explain.

When I looked over, though, his other hand was over the bedside table, holding a glass bottle in his palm. It fit much better in his hand than it did for me, as if moulded to his larger grip. As he read the faded lettering, his finger moved to rest on the spray mechanism.

I didn’t mean to snatch it from him. I didn’t say a word, didn’t even think, I just reached out and pulled it from his hand, my body spiralling from his arm at the same time. My breath came ragged again, my chest rattling as my eyes misted over anew.

There wasn’t much liquid left in the cologne bottle, and I stared at the way it sloshed. My legs hung over the edge of the bed, my back to Miles as a tear splashed against the glass.

“I’m sorry, I wasn’t thinking… Is it from an ex?”

I nodded. “Leon. He…” The mess of fear and guilt in my throat was thick, tough to swallow down. “He died last year.”

Miles went quiet. I wouldn’t know how to respond to that. I wondered if he’d just leave. If he’d stand and make his way from the house and we’d never speak again. The thought made me sob harder.

I felt him rise off the bed and I waited to hear the door shut. Instead, he settled on the floor in front of me, cross legged and eyes incredibly soft. His face was still patient, still kind. The fear came again, wondering when that patience would run out.

But for now, he put a gentle hand on my knee, looked up at me and said, “tell me about Leon.”

My brow still furrowed with the weight of unshed tears, but I stifled my crying, swiped tear tracks from my cheeks and sat a little straighter, looking down at Miles.

“We went to school together…”
