Discovering and Discarding My Inner Art Snob

It may be a strange confession, given I’m studying – rather naively – to write for a living, but the truth is I’ve never been very artistic. One look at my middling or worse report cards from school art classes would be enough proof, particularly the comments from teachers praising my effort in the face of “difficulties” and “frustration” with the work. Even in writing, my most creative outlet, infusing my work with the poetics of language is something that never came easy.

With that in mind, maybe you’ll forgive that galleries never called to me. The art that found its way in front of me wasn’t given the appreciation it deserved. It felt like the paintings placed with reverence on well-lit white walls could sense my lack of interest. And too often I could only stand there. Doomed. Empty eyes glazing over and gazing back with no want or ability to understand.

So, when a university assignment required my immersion into a gallery space, you can imagine my unease. Not just any gallery, either. Not one whose main purpose is mass appeal, to the point where even I could stumble onto some of the meaning on display. No, I would have to be wholly present in an Artist Run Initiative, an ARI. A place by and for artists. Where they start out, facilitated by those who are so dedicated to art that they donate their time. Not exactly my scene.

In preparation to overcome this, I tried to put myself in the headspace of a connoisseur, donning the disguise of a fellow artist. I hoped that in doing so, I’d blend in, pretend I belonged, maybe even make it true. The subject of my infiltration was the Yarra Sculpture Gallery. My maps app told me it was on Vere Street, a minute’s walk off Hoddle, a road I’d trundled down for four years in transit to and from the city. It had been there since 1997 according to their website, four years prior to my birth. Riding along the Hurstbridge train line from Melbourne Central, my nerves really kicked in, gaining traction as I disembarked at Collingwood Station.

The path to YSG took me through the quiet Gahan Reserve. An antsy little dog pulled taut at his lead, desperate to greet me as I tracked along past him. The sun beamed through trees and lit upon the grass. The old, identical houses sat in a line across the road from the park. The placid space soothed me. I lingered on a bench and breathed with the wind.

It’s just a gallery. How much could go wrong?

Imagining myself swirling an expensive glass of wine I don’t drink and twirling an obnoxious moustache I can’t grow, I moved past the grey concrete of a warehouse building and approached the off-white brick edifice of my destination. The logo and name of the gallery printed on the side of the building assured me I was in the right place and the door, propped open with a brick, told me I wouldn’t be immediately turned away.

I moved down a dim hallway, narration echoing from somewhere on the right. At the end of the hall was a wide open room, a woman at a desk off to the side. She didn’t react to my presence. I hoped it was from how I seemed to belong to the space. More likely it was due to my quiet footfalls. The large room was centred on a chair and a few dozen hexagonal mirrors hanging from the high ceiling, walls occupied by displays.

I wandered the walls, squinting and nodding as if it made sense immediately. One section had “Our Lives” written in chalk above a largely blank posterboard, a note beside it inviting me to take up the markers beneath the empty space to write or draw my own contribution. I was suspicious, stuck in my conception of galleries functioning under the “look don’t touch” imperative. Squinting, I moved to the side, paranoia pushing me along.

As I went to read the blurb about the work and the artist, I noticed some raised lettering the same colour as the wall, spelling out “Gallery 2”. My mildly manic looks around the room for Gallery 1 brought attention from the woman at the desk.

She smiled as she introduced herself as Rachel Mackay, and asked me if I knew the artist, Elnaz Nourizadeh. When I said no, she talked me through some of the exhibition as I tried to hide my worries.

“Elnaz wants everybody to sit in this chair for a little bit and really take in the exhibition.” The chair was plush, covered in maroon leather, with one leg replaced by a stack of white-covered books. Rachel told me to come find her when I was done and I took the time to settle myself down. The chair was comfortable and the mirrors hanging from the ceiling were each angled so I couldn’t see my reflection in any of them. They’re all engraved. A lion, a face, a womb, a bat, all scratched onto different mirrors.

I tried to analyse the art as a way back into the snob I was impersonating. I wondered if I was meant to see myself, if a taller or shorter person would be reflected in the engravings, part of the art and part of nature. I wasn’t sure, but at the very least, it put me out of focus for a moment, a thankful reprieve from fixating on how I appeared in this gallery. When I could act like I belonged again, I rose and headed deeper into the building.

Between the back wall of Gallery 2 and a row of glass windows into an office, Rachel introduced me to Gallery 3, where she was putting together a room that was “Drawn From Domesticity”. It took on elements of home as it impacted her creative practice. From her childhood vandalism of her parent’s dictionary by the door, to a recreation of her current-day fridge, complete with a quick sketch of the shelves from a week before. She walked me around the smaller room, explaining that the artists on exhibition were undertaking installations to develop their pieces in the gallery across their run in the space. She tells me it’s a great opportunity to work to the specifics of a space and develop their craft.

Something about Rachel reminded me of my mother. I can’t decide if it’s the short stature and bright smile, the teacherly tone as she walks me around, or the slight disappointment when she urges me to take a guess at something and I get it wrong. I realise after one such failure that my mask of expertise has disappeared. But Rachel doesn’t seem overly perturbed, continuing jovially. When we’d circled the room, I worked up the courage to ask where Gallery 1 was and she pointed me back toward the exit. I felt silly when I found the doorway I had missed, tucked just before the entry hallway opened into the large room of Gallery 2. It’s deeper in this room that the voiceover I heard earlier is emanating from, a small subsection of Gallery 1 labelled as the projector room.

Standing in these rooms without a guide, I again felt lost in the art. It didn’t feel so stifling this time. Having spent time with Rachel, I didn’t feel the need to pretend that I belonged. It was unusual for me, but something I tried to carry into my return to YSG, this time for a meeting with the gallery manager, Andrea Hughes.

Andrea worked behind the windows I had noticed the day before. When I arrived, she was finishing a chat with a man that was leaning against the doorframe to the office space. When she noticed me loitering, she finished the conversation before ushering me in and inviting me to sit in a small chair beside the door.

It was here that Andrea delighted and captivated me. She told me a story.

She told me about how for more than 25 years, YSG has stood as a place for artists at any stage in their life and practice to display their sculpture work. Utilising the space of what was once a factory, it was founded under the fear that the commercial galleries had eradicated affordable spaces for their medium. The founding members have moved on now. “Julie Collins runs the commercial & Gallery in Sorrento now.”

Even without their founders, the gallery continues thanks to the tireless volunteer work that’s put in. Across 8 volunteers, 2 art techs, and a committee board, the only paid position is Andrea’s, as the gallery manager, and even that is only one day per week. Each of them are artists, just there for the sake of the art form. It’s clear how earnest she is when she tells me “it’s a job of love.”

The running of the gallery is a difficult task, one that Andrea’s impressively managed for the last 5 years. “We basically run on nothing… we have to fill the space to keep running” Andrea tells me, explaining how they plan out their exhibitions more than a year in advance. They manage with aid from a number of places. Officeworks and Bunnings offer some free supplies each year, and the peppercorn rent they pay the Yarra City Council certainly goes some way to keeping the gallery running. YSG also source prizes for their annual members show from a variety of providers.

The show is a free event for members of their Contemporary Sculptors Association, a membership that costs $60 a year and is required to run any exhibition at YSG.

The judges and prizes of the member’s show are the embodiment of a theme I noticed in how YSG runs. Andrea told me about how they bring in prestigious people to assess the art like curators for the National Gallery of Victoria or academics in the field. This year, they’ve organised the co-founders of the Melbourne Sculpture Biennale. And for prizes, they regularly give away free exhibitions at their own and other gallery spaces. As Andrea put it, YSG wants to “open a door” for artists that don’t have paths into conventional, commercial exhibitions.

This intention was clear in a number of other topics that came up during the interview. Twice a year, YSG runs two-month residencies at a heavily discounted price. They partner with Arts Access Victoria to run grant-subsidised mentoring at no cost for artists with disability. They host shows for university graduates and invite people in to see the space before committing to an exhibition. Again and again, I was struck by the way YSG seemed to operate. Take on the burdens of administration, prestige and connection with the commercial art world as a means of bringing that world to artists who don’t have a seat at the table yet. A crystallisation of the idea that YSG was founded on.

As we finished up the chat and I thanked her for her time, I looked through the window and noticed Rachel in Gallery 3. I wondered how many of the artists that displayed at YSG felt like they were in the artist’s spaces that seemed so gatekept. How many felt like they had gotten there because of YSG.

On the way out of the office, I found myself looking again at the chair in the centre of Gallery 2, and the mirrors that dangled in front of it. Drawn to it, I sat again, using the chance to take my bag off and stow away my notepad and pen. Once I looked up, however, I was struck by the fact that through the mirrors, I could see almost the entirety of one of the walls.

A checkerboard pattern took up most of it, where beige squares had been tacked onto the white space. The squares were covered in newspaper clippings and article titles, most in all caps and heavy black font. Along the top row, one of the squares was walled in by layers of the word “ESCAPE”, another was crisscrossed with “PROMISES” and “FEAR”. A little further down, in a particularly busy square was a smaller cutting, sticking out from under a few other headlines. “Why Kids Worry”. It’s almost an overwhelming display, amplified by the white negative space that surrounds each section. The checkerboard pattern seems to sag along the bottom of the wall, continuing onto the cement floor of the space. It makes me think of melting or of everything oozing down from where it’s overflowed. On the left side, the outline of the pattern breaks, bent off and away, swirling around the words “What are we fighting for” in chalk. A small table stood next to it all, piles of articles sitting under a pair of scissors and glue sticks. The fodder, ready to be cannibalised for use in the display.

I stood from the chair with my eyes still moving square to square on the grid. I slung my backpack over my shoulder. I took a deep breath. I looked back to the mirrors. In some of their reflections, from this angle, I could see myself.
